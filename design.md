# Simple, bold screens — thirteen principles

> Bold isn't loud. Bold is what's left when you take everything else away.

You earn impact by leaving things out, not piling things on. Thirteen rules below. Each one is short on purpose.

---

## 1. Start with white

**Rule.** Empty space is the loudest thing on the screen.
**Why.** A clean room makes one chair look important. A full room makes every chair look the same.
**Do** — let big margins do the talking.
**Don't** — fill space just because it's there.

## 2. One job per screen

**Rule.** One primary action. Everything else steps back.
**Why.** Two buttons asking equally loud is no button at all.
**Do** — make the next step obvious.
**Don't** — show two primary buttons side by side.

## 3. Shout once

**Rule.** Pick one thing to be big. The rest stays quiet.
**Why.** If everything is big, nothing is.
**Do** — one headline, one number, one hero — your choice, but only one.
**Don't** — set three things at size 48 and hope the user picks.

## 4. Weight before colour

**Rule.** Heavy type carries emphasis before colour does.
**Why.** Bold reads on every screen, every light, every eye. Weight is for importance; colour is for kind.
**Do** — bold the important word.
**Don't** — colour a word red and call it emphasis.

## 5. Colour does a job

**Rule.** A small palette, each colour with a role. Used flat. Used big.
**Why.** Commit to a colour and it carries meaning. Wash it down and it carries nothing.
**Do** — flood the sidebar, fill the section header, paint the status pill.
**Don't** — gradients, tints, or 60% opacity to take the edge off.

## 6. Plain words, verbs first

**Rule.** Say what happens. In words a tired person knows.
**Why.** Clear copy looks like clear design.
**Do** — "Pay", "Send", "Start".
**Don't** — "Submit transaction", "Proceed to confirmation".

## 7. Read like an F

**Rule.** Left-align text. Centre only buttons and short labels.
**Why.** Eyes hunt down the left edge. Help them.
**Do** — keep paragraphs flush left.
**Don't** — justify text, or centre paragraphs for "balance".

## 8. Stay on the grid

**Rule.** Space things on multiples of eight.
**Why.** A grid gives rhythm. Rhythm feels calm without you noticing.
**Do** — 8, 16, 24, 32, 40.
**Don't** — 7, 13, 19, "looks about right".

## 9. Clear beats clever

**Rule.** Icons and labels a stranger gets in one second.
**Why.** Your screen will be opened on the bus, at 2am, in a second language.
**Do** — a plain envelope means "mail".
**Don't** — a metaphor that needs a caption.

## 10. Have a point of view

**Rule.** Say what you think. Quietly. Once.
**Why.** A screen with no opinion is a screen no one remembers.
**Do** — pick a side. Let the layout show it.
**Don't** — hedge with carousels, testimonials, and badges to feel safe.

## 11. Cut, then cut again

**Rule.** If you must add, remove two things first.
**Why.** Simple isn't a starting point. It's what's left after the third edit.
**Do** — ship the version you're slightly nervous is too sparse.
**Don't** — keep the box, the badge, the help text, and the tooltip "just in case".

## 12. Serve the task, not the app

**Rule.** Cut UI that exists for the app's sake, not the user's.
**Why.** Tabs, breadcrumbs, toolbars, account menus, settings gears — that's the app talking to itself. The user came to do one thing; let that thing be the page.
**Do** — push the work to the centre; tuck housekeeping into a corner or behind a key.
**Don't** — wrap every screen in a header, sidebar, and footer "for consistency".

## 13. The output is the input

**Rule.** Let the user act on the thing itself.
**Why.** A lot of chrome exists because the content isn't touchable. If the title renames on click and the card drags to move, the screen needs less furniture around it.
**Do** — make the content the interaction surface; reveal controls on hover or focus. Physical metaphors earn their place when they ARE the interaction — a card you can drag, fold, stick.
**Don't** — wrap every object in a toolbar in case the user wants to do something. Don't bolt on materiality (shadows, folds, textures) as decoration.

## Icons

The principles above apply to icons too. The specifics below — strokes, fills, the no-skeuomorphism rule — govern icons. UI surfaces answer to the thirteen above, not this section.

**Shape.**
- Geometric. Built from circles, squares, rounded rectangles. Nothing organic.
- Symmetric where you can. Asymmetry needs a reason.
- One body per icon. No floating dots, no decorative ticks.
- Drawn on a 24×24 grid with 2pt of padding. 20×20 live area.

**Style — pick one and stay.**
- *Outlined:* 1.5–2pt stroke. Rounded caps and joins. Single weight throughout — no thick-and-thin.
- *Filled:* one solid colour. No internal holes that fragment the shape.
- Don't mix the two. Unless filled = selected and outlined = default. That's the only excuse.

**Corners.** Small radius, 1–2pt. Matches the typeface. Mitred corners — never.

**Weight that matches the type.**
- Regular body type → 1.5pt icons.
- Heavier type → 2pt icons.
- Icons and headlines should feel like siblings, not strangers.

**Optical, not mathematical.** A circle the same height as a square looks smaller. Make the circle a touch bigger so it carries equal weight. Boring craft work — it's why a polished set looks calm.

**No-gos.**
- No gradients.
- No drop shadows, inner shadows, glows.
- No duotone.
- No 3D, no perspective, no skeuomorphism. A card is a rectangle, not a tiny credit card.
- No outlined icon with a filled accent dot. Clever, not clear.

**The squint test.** Print the set at 16pt. Squint. If the silhouette still tells you what it is, you're done.

**Core set — about twenty covers most product screens.**
- *Navigation:* arrow-left, arrow-right, chevron-down, close, menu, more
- *Actions:* plus, minus, search, edit, trash, share, download, check
- *Status:* info, warning, success
- *Object:* home, person, gear, bell, envelope, card, document, calendar, lock, eye

**Pair every icon with a label.** The only icons a stranger really gets without words are: close, back, search, menu. Everything else needs the label.

---

## The one idea behind all thirteen

Simple and bold sound opposed. They aren't.
**Simple** is fewer things on the screen.
**Bold** is the one thing left being unmistakable — a headline, a number, a wall of colour.
You can't have the second without the first.
